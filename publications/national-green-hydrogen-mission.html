National Green Hydrogen Mission

Author: Mohit Kumar Editor: Riya Pawar Project Urvi  ·  Academia for Sustainable Development
← Back to Publications

Introduction

The National Green Hydrogen Mission aims to position India as a global leader in green hydrogen production and utilization. By targeting over 8 lakh crore in investments and 6 lakh new jobs by 2030, the mission promises significant economic growth through domestic manufacturing and reduced imports. This will aid India's decarbonization and energy diversification, with a potential abatement of 50 million tonnes of annual emissions and supporting the renewable capacity addition of 125 GW. Additionally, green hydrogen supports energy storage for a consistent renewable power supply.

The mission promises economic, environmental, and energy security upsides - spurring India's energy self-sufficiency and growth while enabling cleaner industries through renewable integration and reduced fossil fuel dependency worth over 1 lakh crore in imports. With a focus on reducing petroleum use, greenhouse gas emissions, and air pollution, the mission seeks to foster a diverse and efficient energy infrastructure by promoting the widespread adoption of hydrogen and fuel cell technologies.

Green hydrogen, produced using renewable energy, can facilitate this transition in hard-to-decarbonize sectors. The Government of India has furthered the National Green Hydrogen Mission to scale up the production and utilization of green hydrogen across the economy. This policy analysis delves into the key goals and objectives, the current status of the mission, its impact, and the challenges ahead.

Aims and Objectives

The overarching goal of the National Green Hydrogen Mission is to make India the global hub for the production, utilization, and export of green hydrogen and its derivatives, reducing India's dependency on imported fuels.

  • Build capabilities to produce at least 5 million metric tonnes (MMT) per annum of green hydrogen by 2030, with the potential to reach 10 MMT with the growth of export markets.
  • Support the replacement of grey hydrogen with green hydrogen in sectors like ammonia production, petroleum refining, city gas networks, and steel manufacturing.
  • Facilitate exports of green hydrogen and derivatives like green ammonia and green methanol, targeting approximately 10% share of the expected global demand of over 100 MMT by 2030.
  • Make India a global manufacturing hub for electrolyzers - targeting 60 GW per year of electrolyzer manufacturing capacity, of which 15 GW per year is expected to be supported by production-linked incentives.
  • Promote R&D, pilot projects, and infrastructure development across the green hydrogen value chain spanning renewable energy, electrolysis, storage, and transportation.

Current Status

The budgetary allocations for the mission are distributed among key areas, namely the Strategic Interventions for Green Hydrogen Transition (SIGHT) program - encompassing incentives for the manufacturing of electrolyzers and the production of green hydrogen - alongside pilot projects in steel, mobility, and shipping, R&D, and other essential components.

India currently produces around 6.7 million metric tonnes of hydrogen per year, used across sectors like oil refining, ammonia and fertilizer production, methanol, and metals manufacturing. However, over 60% of hydrogen demand comes from the refining sector alone, and most current production is based on fossil fuel routes - yielding almost 6.5 million tonnes of carbon-intensive grey hydrogen. Environment-friendly green hydrogen accounts for less than 1% of production today.

India's green hydrogen standard is set at 2 kg of CO₂ equivalent per kg of hydrogen - positioned between the EU's 3.2 kg and the Green Hydrogen Organization's recommendation of 1 kg, balancing climate concerns with global market competitiveness.

Gujarat has emerged as a frontrunner with clusters of operational plants. Reliance is building mega renewable complexes to produce solar equipment, batteries, fuel cells, electrolyzers, and green hydrogen. GAIL is piloting hydrogen blending in natural gas. NTPC has innovated with a green hydrogen fuelling station in Leh. Oil India Limited has a small operational pilot in Assam. Indian Oil is integrating green hydrogen production at its Mathura refinery and building a standalone unit at Kochi airport.

Post-Implementation Prospects

A government report forecasts that scaling domestic green hydrogen to 10 million tons per year in the next decade could generate over 6 million new jobs across transport, gas, and manufacturing industries. Steel, fertilizer, and petrochemical firms are expected to see falling production costs and increased output. India's state-owned natural gas companies are also investing heavily in green hydrogen blending up to 10% into existing gas pipelines.

Research has shown that decentralized green hydrogen projects can create jobs and skills training for women at the community level. Green hydrogen-based clean cooking alternatives can disproportionately benefit rural women by freeing them from unclean cooking duties - an intervention with documented positive educational and health impacts.

End-use cases in heavy vehicles and data centres are already emerging in initial pilot studies. The mission is expected to drive down electrolyzer costs by up to 60% through manufacturing incentives and scale, making small-scale electrolysis viable across industries like metals, glass, electronics, oil and gas, and textiles.

Challenges

Achieving the mission's production target of 5 MMT necessitates an addition of approximately 125 GW of renewable energy capacity - a challenge when many state-owned distribution companies are grappling with substantial financial losses.

  • Nearly 99% of current hydrogen demand originates from refining and fertilizer sectors, both of which require significant transition capital.
  • The allocated financial resources of Rs 19,744 crore constitute only about 2.5% of the overall investment target of Rs 8 lakh crore, underlining significant viability gaps.
  • India presently lacks large-scale manufacturing facilities for alkaline or PEM electrolyzers, leading to dependence on imports.
  • The absence of robust storage and transportation infrastructure for hydrogen poses a critical gap that could impede the scaling-up process.
  • Current costs of green hydrogen are four to five times higher than carbon-generating alternatives, raising affordability concerns.

Conclusion

The National Green Hydrogen Mission marks a pivotal step in advancing India's energy security and sustainability goals. By targeting 5 MMT annual green hydrogen production and positioning India as a major exporting hub by 2030, it lays the foundation for large-scale decarbonization of carbon-intensive sectors. If executed efficiently, the mission can attract over 8 lakh crore in investments, create 6 lakh jobs, and lead to 50 MMT emissions reductions annually.

However, the policy's success remains contingent on ensuring robust renewable energy expansion, maintaining technology cost-competitiveness, and creating export linkages. To enable the ambitious production target, India must promote decentralized renewable energy systems, provide financial incentives to improve economic viability, develop national-scale hydrogen storage and distribution infrastructure, foster international collaborations, and incentivize private investments across the entire value chain.
