Green India Mission

Author: Soham Chakraborty Editor: Riya Pawar Project Urvi  ·  Academia for Sustainable Development

Introduction

Climate change poses an imminent threat to humanity - melting polar ice caps, hunger, displacement of resources, loss of species, health risks, severe storms, and rising sea levels all demand immediate attention. In response, the then Prime Minister of India introduced the National Action Plan on Climate Change (NAPCC) on 30th June 2008, a national strategy focused on adapting to climate change and promoting ecological sustainability while maintaining economic growth.

The NAPCC consists of eight missions covering various sectors. One of these, the Green India Mission (GIM), is devoted to reviving and safeguarding India's depleting vegetation. Initiated in February 2014, the GIM's goals are to increase forest and tree cover, improve ecosystem services, enhance carbon sequestration, and increase forest revenues.

This report examines the Green India Mission's objectives, strategies, and achievements since inception, drawing from multiple data sources and existing literature to assess progress.

Achievements of the Green India Mission

The Green India Mission was launched to mobilise over Rs 60,000 crore for forestry initiatives. From 2008 to 2022, India's forest cover increased from 21.23% to 24.62%, putting the nation on track for its 33% target by 2030.

  • The government implemented the Joint Forest Management (JFM) program, engaging over 2.5 million forest dwellers in forest management activities. By 2010, over 112,896 committees managed approximately 2.46 million km² of wooded land, with around 14.5 million families benefiting.
  • The National Forestry Information System (NFIS) was established as a centralised platform for managing and monitoring forest data, integrating satellite images, GIS, and ground surveys.
  • Over 12 million hectares of non-forest land have been reforested, restoring degraded landscapes.
  • The mission promotes sustainable management practices - ensuring responsible timber harvesting while protecting biodiversity, soil, and water resources.
  • Community ownership has been strengthened: over 2.5 million forest dwellers actively participate in JFM programmes, generating employment particularly in rural areas.
India's forest cover grew from 21.23% to 24.62% between 2008 and 2022, well on track for the 33% target by 2030 - demonstrating measurable progress from the GIM and related forestry programmes.

Evaluation

While the GIM has undeniably boosted forest cover, critics argue that it prioritises quantity over ecological quality. Planting incompatible species like eucalyptus in unsuitable regions, ignoring weather and soil conditions, can exacerbate drought and threaten local biodiversity. This concern is reflected in the mission's shortfall in 2015-16 and 2016-17, where it fell 34% short of its target - covering only 44,000 of the planned 67,000 hectares.

Beyond ecological concerns, encroachments fuelled by illegal construction and logging demand stricter enforcement. Limited financial resources often hinder sustainable forest management practices. Addressing these multifaceted challenges requires a holistic approach combining stringent regulatory measures, community education, and increased financial investments.

Moving forward, GIM needs to prioritise species selection based on local ecosystems, address resource constraints, and strengthen enforcement to ensure its green ambitions translate into lasting environmental benefits.

Conclusion

The National Mission for a Green India has made significant progress in restoring and protecting India's forests. The mission increases forest cover, promotes sustainable forestry practices, and empowers communities to act as stewards of their local ecosystems. The broader NAPCC framework represents India's most comprehensive attempt to align development goals with climate resilience.

However, challenges such as species selection quality, financial constraints, encroachments, and limited public awareness remain. Addressing these and enhancing the implementation of forest management strategies is critical to achieving the mission's objectives and India's long-term climate commitments.
