Climate Refugees: The World's Forgotten Victims

Author: Tanavi Gumma Editor: Riya Pawar Project Urvi  ·  Academia for Sustainable Development
← Back to Publications

Who Are Climate Refugees?

The term "climate refugees" has been used since 1985, when UNEP expert Essam El-Hinnawi defined them - also called climate or environmental migrants - as people who have been "forced to leave their traditional habitat, temporarily or permanently, because of marked environmental disruption." However, the extent of the definition continues to be ambiguous.

Under the 1951 Convention Relating to the Status of Refugees, a "refugee" is defined as a person who has crossed an international border owing to a well-founded fear of persecution for reasons of race, religion, nationality, or political opinion. Climate change mostly causes internal displacement before escalating to cross-border movement - meaning climate refugees largely fall outside the legal protections afforded to conventional refugees. These people face greater political risks, limited access to human rights protection, and the threat of deportation.

Climate-related migration and displacement is set to be the greatest humanitarian challenge of our era - yet it remains systematically overlooked and underestimated in international policy frameworks.

The Scale of the Crisis

Climate-induced displacement is already accelerating. In a single recent year, 16.1 million people were displaced due to climate-related factors. It is estimated that by 2050, between 150 and 200 million people will be at risk of being forced to leave their homes as a result of desertification, rising sea levels, and extreme weather conditions.

This is not just an issue for fragile states. The changing patterns of human migration mean that more people across the world are forced to flee their communities in search of safe havens. It is the world's most vulnerable people - those least responsible for causing climate change - who bear the heaviest burden and are least equipped to cope with its consequences.

The Mechanisms of Displacement

Global warming, primarily caused by burning fossil fuels and deforestation, results in rising temperatures and the melting of glaciers and ice caps. The IPCC predicts a sea-level rise of 0.18 to 0.6 metres between 1990 and 2100, with severe implications for low-lying coastal regions worldwide.

  • Bangladesh - Half the population lives within 5 metres above sea level. Rising seas have already submerged areas like Bhola Island, displacing 500,000 people. A potential loss of 17% of Bangladesh's land by 2050 could force around 20 million climate refugees.
  • The Maldives - With its highest point just 2.4 metres above sea level, the nation faces existential threat. Sea-level rise endangers its tourism-dependent economy and has prompted government plans for potential evacuation.
  • Venice, Italy - Built on islands in a lagoon, the city experiences increasing flooding that damages infrastructure, cultural sites, and its tourism economy.
  • Coastal cities globally - Manhattan, London, Shanghai, Hamburg, Bangkok, Jakarta, Mumbai, Manila, and Buenos Aires are all susceptible to sea-level rise over the coming decades.
  • Sub-Saharan Africa and the Horn of Africa - Severe drought forces subsistence farmers to abandon their land. In Kenya and Somalia, worsening droughts, compounded by conflict, drive both internal displacement and cross-border refugee movements.
  • Inland regions near the Gobi Desert and the Sahara - Productive land is lost annually to desertification, driving migration to urban centres and intensifying pressure on city infrastructure.
The interplay between climate change, conflict, poverty, and persecution greatly increases the complexity of refugee emergencies. In Somalia, worsening droughts combined with Al-Shabab-related violence drive displacement simultaneously - making it increasingly impossible to separate "climate" from "political" refugees.

Health Impacts

The climate crisis is affecting individuals and global public health systems in almost every region of the world. This is particularly evident in rising malnutrition due to droughts and the increasing incidence of water-borne diseases such as cholera due to floods - which negatively affect already weakened health systems, particularly in low- and middle-income countries.

Refugees and migrants have specific physical and mental health needs linked to their exposure to climate and environmental conditions. They face significant health risks before, during, and after displacement, with access to primary care and continuity of health services often disrupted by migration, weak health system capacity, financial barriers, and numerous other stressors including abuse and exploitation.

Long-term exposure to pollutants from climate-related industrial disruption also compounds chronic disease risks in displaced communities, creating multi-generational public health consequences that are rarely accounted for in displacement statistics.

Why the World Must Act

The scale of the challenge demands urgent, coordinated global action. International humanitarian agencies are already overwhelmed, increasingly stretched, and underfunded. If internal displacements that are accelerating today begin turning into large-scale international refugee movements, the geopolitical, humanitarian, and economic consequences will be unprecedented.

It is important to understand that change begins from within - efficient policies can only be successfully implemented if individuals, communities, businesses, and governments all contribute. Every person who understands the gravity of the issue, reduces their own footprint, and advocates for climate-responsible policy is part of the solution.

We need to take rigorous steps before the displacements that are already happening within countries become irreversible international refugee crises. The people most affected are not the ones who caused this problem. The moral and practical imperative to act has never been clearer.
