Plastic Pollution in India's Rivers: A 2025 Data Snapshot

Project Urvi Research Team  ·  projecturvi.in

India is home to some of the world's most ecologically vital river systems — but also some of its most plastic-burdened ones. In 2025, our team analysed available plastic load data across 14 major river systems, combining field survey data, satellite analysis, and secondary datasets to produce one of the most detailed snapshots of riverine plastic pollution in South Asia to date.

Key finding: 6 of the 14 rivers surveyed showed plastic concentration levels more than 3x the South Asian average during peak months, with the Yamuna, Sabarmati, and Cooum among the most severely affected.

Methodology

Data was gathered through three primary channels: published field survey reports from 2023–2025, satellite-based surface plastic detection data (CSIR-NEERI and global datasets), and direct outreach to municipal bodies and local NGOs operating in the surveyed basins.

Plastic concentration was computed as tonnes per kilometre of river stretch surveyed, normalised for seasonal flow volume. We defined "hotspots" as segments exceeding 2.5 tonnes/km during low-flow season.

Key findings

  • Total estimated plastic load across the 14 surveyed rivers exceeded 1.2 million tonnes in 2025.
  • Urban river segments — particularly those passing through cities with populations above 1 million — accounted for 73% of total plastic load.
  • Seasonal variation was significant: plastic concentration was on average 2.1x higher during low-rainfall months (Oct–Jan) versus peak monsoon (Jul–Sep).
  • Single-use plastics (SUP) — primarily packaging films, bottles, and sachets — constituted approximately 61% of identifiable plastic debris by weight.

Data limitations: satellite detection has a resolution floor that likely undercounts microplastics. Figures should be treated as conservative estimates.

What this means for plastic recovery

The concentration and type profile of riverine plastics in India has direct implications for recovery economics. High proportions of packaging films and rigid PET — among the most commercially valuable recyclable streams — suggest that targeted recovery operations at key hotspot locations could be both environmentally impactful and commercially viable.

Opportunity: Targeted recovery at the top 10 hotspot segments alone could divert an estimated 280,000 tonnes of plastic annually from river systems — material that could be processed for industrial supply.

Policy implications

Our analysis supports a zoned intervention approach: rather than attempting river-wide collection (which is operationally prohibitive), policymakers and recovery operators should focus investment on the 15–20% of river stretches that account for the majority of plastic load. In tandem, upstream source reduction — particularly targeting urban drainage infrastructure — remains essential for long-term impact.

We are continuing to update this dataset quarterly. If your organisation works on plastic recovery, policy, or environmental monitoring in the region, we welcome collaboration.
