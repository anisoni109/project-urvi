Temperature Anomalies in South Asia: A Decade of Change (2015-2025)

Project Urvi Research Team  ·  projecturvi.in

The past decade has made climate change in South Asia increasingly legible — not as a future risk, but as a present-tense reality recorded in temperature data. This analysis presents ten years of temperature anomaly data across the South Asian region (2015–2025), disaggregated by sub-region, to show where warming has been fastest, where it has been most disruptive, and what the trajectory suggests for the decade ahead.

Understanding temperature anomaly

Temperature anomaly measures the deviation of a given period's average temperature from a historical baseline (here: the 1951–1980 average, consistent with IPCC and NASA GISS methodology). A positive anomaly means the period was warmer than the baseline; a negative anomaly means it was cooler. We use this measure because it allows meaningful comparison across regions with very different baseline climates.

The ten-year picture

Across South Asia as a whole, the average annual temperature anomaly in 2025 was +1.3°C above the 1951–1980 baseline — up from +0.7°C in 2015. This trajectory of approximately +0.06°C per year is consistent with global warming trends but sits at the higher end of regional projections made a decade ago.

The five hottest years on record in South Asia (measured by average temperature anomaly) have all occurred since 2019. 2024 was the warmest year in the regional record.

Sub-regional variation

  • Northwest India and Pakistan: The fastest-warming sub-region. Average anomaly in 2025: +1.8°C. The 2022 Pakistan floods and repeated severe heatwaves in northwest India are consistent with this trend.
  • Indo-Gangetic Plain: +1.5°C anomaly. Summer heat stress is compounding into multi-day events with wet-bulb temperatures approaching survivability limits for outdoor workers.
  • Coastal South India and Sri Lanka: +1.1°C anomaly — lower, but compounded by sea surface temperature rise affecting monsoon patterns.
  • Himalayan and Hindu Kush arc: +2.1°C anomaly — the highest of any sub-region. Accelerated glacier retreat is already affecting freshwater availability for hundreds of millions of people downstream.
  • Bangladesh and Eastern India: +1.2°C anomaly, compounded by increasing cyclone intensity and salinity intrusion in delta regions.

What the trajectory suggests

If the current rate of warming continues without significant mitigation, South Asia is on track to reach average anomalies of +2°C above the pre-industrial baseline by the early 2030s — a threshold associated with significantly increased climate risk for food systems, water resources, and human health across the region. The Himalayan sub-region may cross this threshold sooner.

It is important to note that these are regional averages. Localised extremes — heat events, flash floods, glacial lake outbursts — are already exceeding what these averages suggest in specific locations and specific years.

Why this matters for sustainability strategy

For businesses and policymakers in South Asia, this data has direct operational implications. Climate risk is no longer adequately modelled using historical weather patterns. Physical risk assessments, supply chain resilience planning, and community engagement programmes need to incorporate warming trajectories explicitly. We support organisations in embedding this kind of analysis into their sustainability strategy and risk frameworks.
