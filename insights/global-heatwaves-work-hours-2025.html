Global Heatwaves and Lost Work Hours: A 2025 Snapshot

Project Urvi Research Team  ·  projecturvi.in

Extreme heat is no longer a seasonal anomaly - it is becoming a structural economic risk. In 2025, heatwave exposure intensified across South Asia, the Middle East, parts of Africa, Southern Europe, and Latin America, with cascading impacts on labour productivity, health systems, and urban energy demand. Outdoor and non-air-conditioned indoor workers remain the most exposed, particularly in construction, logistics, agriculture, and manufacturing.

Key finding: a small number of extreme-heat geographies account for a disproportionate share of productivity losses, especially where high humidity coincides with low access to cooling infrastructure.

Schematic regional map highlighting where heat exposure, humidity, and outdoor work concentration overlap most strongly.

The strongest overlap of heat exposure and labour vulnerability remains concentrated in South Asia, Sub-Saharan Africa, the Middle East, Latin America, and Southern Europe. These regions differ in climate, but they share one practical trait: a large share of work still depends on outdoor conditions, weak cooling access, or overstretched urban infrastructure.

Why heat now behaves like infrastructure risk

Heat impacts propagate through systems. As daytime peaks intensify, urban grids face sharp demand spikes from cooling. Transit systems slow, logistics windows shrink, water demand rises, and public-health services absorb more cases of heat stress, dehydration, and cardiovascular strain. For employers, the cost is not only absenteeism but also lower effective work intensity and compressed operating schedules.

That is why heat increasingly behaves less like weather and more like infrastructure stress. Regions with weak distribution grids, low cooling penetration, poor building envelopes, and limited worker protection experience much larger productivity losses than regions with similar air temperatures but better adaptive capacity. The operational question is no longer whether heat will occur, but how exposed an economy is once it does.

Which sectors absorb the largest operational hit

Construction and logistics typically register the earliest productivity losses because work is fixed to daytime windows and often occurs on heat-retaining surfaces such as concrete, asphalt, ports, and industrial yards. Agriculture faces a different profile: output may hold for a period, but worker fatigue, irrigation pressure, and crop-quality losses rise together. Manufacturing is often treated as less exposed, yet in many lower-income regions the most affected facilities are lightly cooled, labour-intensive, and highly sensitive to even short interruptions.

These sector differences matter for planning. A city with a large warehousing or construction base requires different resilience measures than one dominated by office employment. Heat action plans need to align labour rules, grid investments, emergency care, and urban design with the actual structure of local employment.

Where policy needs to shift

  • Mandatory heat-action plans for cities with recurrent extreme-heat days.
  • Labour protection standards tied to wet-bulb temperature thresholds.
  • Cooling access as a resilience issue, not only an energy issue.
  • Public investment in shade, reflective materials, and early-warning systems.

For businesses, the implications are immediate: redesign work windows, harden facilities, and integrate heat exposure into operational planning. In climate-vulnerable regions, heat is now a balance-sheet issue.

What organisations should track next

The best leading indicators are no longer annual temperature averages alone. Organisations should track wet-bulb exposure, nighttime minimum temperatures, feeder-level power reliability during peak heat, cooling access among vulnerable workers, and the share of operations taking place in outdoor or poorly ventilated environments. These indicators explain real disruption more clearly than headline temperature alone.

For policymakers, the next frontier is integration: heat planning should connect labour ministries, health departments, electricity utilities, and municipal authorities. Heatwaves are now a multi-system governance problem, and fragmented institutions are increasingly the main barrier to adaptation.

What the rest of the world still misses about heat risk

Much of the global discussion still focuses on daytime peak temperature, but nighttime heat is often the more decisive stressor. When overnight temperatures stay high, workers, children, and elderly people do not physiologically recover. Productivity falls further the next day, health risks accumulate across multiple days, and electricity demand remains elevated around the clock rather than only in afternoon peaks.

Another blind spot is informal labour. A large share of the world's heat-exposed workforce is not protected by formal occupational rules, employer-provided cooling, or paid leave. Street vendors, day labourers, waste pickers, delivery riders, farm workers, and small workshop operators are often missing from the datasets that shape policy debates, even though they absorb some of the heaviest losses.

Why heat inequality is widening

Heat does not hit all neighbourhoods equally. Informal settlements and low-income districts tend to have less tree cover, poorer ventilation, denser roofing materials, weaker power reliability, and less access to affordable cooling. As a result, two households in the same city can experience very different heat realities even under the same weather alert.

This is why heat adaptation should not be framed as a luxury urban-design agenda. It is a question of public health, labour rights, and economic resilience. Cities that fail to address heat inequality are likely to see higher mortality, lower productivity, and greater social stress as extreme heat becomes more frequent.
