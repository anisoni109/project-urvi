The ESG Reporting Gap: What Companies in South Asia Are Missing

Project Urvi Research Team  ·  projecturvi.in

ESG reporting is now a baseline expectation for publicly listed companies in most developed markets. But across South Asia, a significant and under-examined gap persists — not merely in the quantity of disclosures, but in their quality, consistency, and scope. Our review of 200+ corporate ESG disclosures from companies headquartered in India, Bangladesh, Pakistan, and Sri Lanka, published between 2023 and 2025, reveals systemic and recurring gaps that have direct implications for investors, regulators, and business partners.

What we reviewed

We analysed standalone ESG or sustainability reports, annual report sustainability sections, and Business Responsibility and Sustainability Reports (BRSR) for 214 companies across 11 sectors, covering a range of market capitalisation sizes. Disclosures were evaluated against GRI, SASB, and TCFD frameworks as reference standards.

The three biggest gaps

1. Scope 3 emissions — almost universally absent

Only 11% of companies reviewed disclosed any Scope 3 emissions data. Of those, fewer than half used recognised calculation methodologies. For companies with complex supply chains — textiles, electronics, food processing — this omission means that the most material portion of their carbon footprint is simply invisible in their public reporting.

Scope 3 emissions typically represent 70–90% of a company's total carbon footprint. Omitting them renders climate-related disclosures structurally misleading for investors and analysts.

2. Supply chain transparency

68% of companies disclosed no meaningful information about their direct supplier environmental or social performance. Among the 32% that did, disclosures were largely qualitative ("we work with responsible suppliers") with no data on supplier audits, corrective actions, or tier 2 visibility. This is a particular concern in sectors like garments, chemicals, and construction materials where supply chain impacts frequently exceed direct operational impacts.

3. Social impact measurement

Community and social impact sections were the most inconsistently reported area. Many companies reported CSR spending (required under Indian law) as a proxy for social impact, without any outcome measurement. Labour rights, gender equity, and occupational safety data — when present — were often aggregated at a level that obscured meaningful analysis.

What good looks like

Among the companies reviewed, a small cluster (roughly 15%) demonstrated significantly higher disclosure quality. Common characteristics: third-party assurance of key metrics, use of standardised frameworks (BRSR Core or GRI Standards), multi-year trend data, and explicit acknowledgement of data limitations. These companies were disproportionately those with significant export revenues or international institutional investor bases — suggesting that market pressure, rather than regulation, is currently the strongest driver of quality.

What this means in practice

For investors conducting ESG due diligence in South Asia, this analysis suggests that disclosed metrics should be treated with significant scepticism without independent verification. For companies looking to improve their ESG standing, Scope 3 methodology and supply chain transparency represent the highest-leverage areas for authentic improvement.
