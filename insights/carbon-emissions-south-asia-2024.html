Visualising Carbon Emissions by Sector: South Asia 2024

Project Urvi Research Team  ·  projecturvi.in

South Asia is among the world's most climate-vulnerable regions, yet its emissions profile remains poorly understood at the sector level — particularly across smaller economies in the bloc. In this analysis, we present a sector-by-sector breakdown of carbon emissions across South Asia for 2024, drawing on available national energy and industrial statistics, IEA regional estimates, and World Bank indicators.

Emissions by sector: the headline picture

  • Energy & power generation: 38% of total regional emissions — driven largely by coal-heavy power mixes in India, Pakistan, and Bangladesh.
  • Industry (manufacturing, cement, steel): 26% — with India's steel and cement sectors among the highest intensity per unit output globally.
  • Transport: 14% — road transport dominating; aviation is a small but rapidly growing contributor.
  • Agriculture & land use: 13% — methane from livestock and rice paddies forming a significant non-CO₂ share.
  • Buildings & residential: 9% — driven by cooling demand, biomass combustion, and urban growth.
Notably, per capita emissions across South Asia remain 60–70% below the global average — but aggregate growth is accelerating, with total regional emissions up 8.4% year-on-year between 2022 and 2024.

Where things are improving

Renewable energy capacity additions have been the clearest area of improvement. India added approximately 28 GW of solar and wind capacity in 2024, and renewable penetration in the power mix reached 23% — up from 18% in 2022. Sri Lanka showed the most significant year-on-year intensity improvement across all South Asian economies, driven by hydro recovery and efficiency policy.

Where acceleration is a concern

Transport emissions grew fastest of any sector at +11.2% in 2024. Cement production — a key driver of industrial emissions — expanded rapidly in Bangladesh and Pakistan in line with infrastructure development programmes. Both trends suggest that without sector-specific policy intervention, aggregate emissions will continue to rise even as renewable energy scales.

Implications for sustainability strategy

For businesses operating in South Asia, this data highlights two practical priorities: energy sourcing (transition to renewable procurement where possible) and supply chain carbon intensity (particularly for companies sourcing from cement, steel, or transport-heavy sectors). ESG reporting frameworks that treat South Asia as a homogeneous region risk missing the significant variance between countries and sectors that this data reveals.
